### What Likely Happened

Solar panels themselves can survive nearby strikes, but the **inverter and charge controller** are extremely vulnerable to surge damage — even from induced voltage spikes on the wiring from a strike that didn't hit the panels directly. In many cases people lose the electronics while the panels themselves are physically intact but electrically dead due to shorted bypass diodes.

### Why Wind May Actually Be Smarter for You Right Now

Given your observations of regular 30+ km/h winds in winter and spring, a small wind turbine could genuinely serve as a useful bridge power source, and it has some practical advantages in your situation:

- **Simpler lightning protection** — one tower to ground properly vs. a whole roof array with multiple strings
- **Works at night** — supplements whatever storage you have around the clock on windy days
- **Complements your future solar** — when you do reinvest in solar, wind covers the winter/spring gap perfectly
- **Lower profile** — a properly grounded tower is a much more straightforward lightning protection problem than a large panel array

### Lessons for Your Future Solar Reinvestment

When you do go back to solar, the protection stack worth investing in:

**Lightning/Surge Protection:**
- Proper grounding of the array frame
- **Type 1 surge protectors** on the DC side (between panels and inverter)
- **Type 2 surge protectors** on the AC side (at the inverter output)
- Isolation transformers for particularly sensitive equipment

**Hail Protection:**
- Panels rated **IEC 61215** have passed the standard hail test (25mm ice balls at 23 m/s), but that's a minimum — look for panels tested to larger sizes
- **Tilt angle matters** — a steeper tilt sheds hail impact energy better than a flat mount
- Some installers in hail-prone areas now use **tempered glass with anti-reflective coating** rated for larger impacts
- **Hail guards** — wire mesh or polycarbonate screens — are an option for smaller arrays

**Insurance:**
- Make sure your homeowner's policy explicitly covers solar equipment for both lightning and hail — many standard policies have gaps here
